ChildSafe

Designed and developed a Web-based application for emergency plans and three types of users: kindergartens, emergency management and state administrators.

the story workflow

A little trivia

I come from a culture that cultivates straight and direct communication, and I appreciate when people openly express their emotions. That's why I listened when emergency managers consistently complained about emergency plans.

Problem 1

According to the law, kindergartens have to submit emergency plans to the emergency management. They would send them by mail, or deliver them in person. Sometimes their plans were handwritten.

But the intent of the law - to help kindergartens better prepare for emergencies, better account for children’s safety, and better communicate with the local emergency management agency - was not fulfilled.

Problem 2

The local agency was obliged to accept the plans and store them in an archive. However, they couldn’t use the details when they deploy during emergencies, and having access to these details can make a difference between life and death.

Their frustration was also tied to their inability to purse their larger strategic goal. During disasters, the agency is supposed to help with coordinating efforts on the ground and that includes providing accurate information to first responders.

For instance, if we have an emergency in the zip codes 40205 and 40207, knowing emergency details would help with deciding what kind of resources are needed on the ground - ambulances, fire trucks, people, etc. It could also help with planning for future emergencies.

Problem 3

State administrators also had a problem on their hands. In order to issue kindergarten licenses, child care administrators had to check if kindergartens complied with the law. To accomplish that, they had to come to the archive room and sift through emergency plans. This was not their only responsibility. It was one of many.

Problems Solved

So this simple application was a win-win for everyone involved.

Kindergartens and preschools can now create and make changes to their emergency plans whenever they want. They are not tied to any place, time, or a particular device.

Emergency managers can view information that can help with preventing injuries, saving lives and reuniting parents with their children. They can do that quickly and easily. They are also not tied to any place, time or a device.

State administrators can easily check if kindergartens comply with the law, and concentrate on much more important business.

Parents can also easily get the copies of emergency plans whenever they ask for it.

Lesson

As they say, it's not what you know, but what you do with what you know.

In this case, users didn’t have to deal with cumbersome technology that causes frustration and anxiety. I knew I didn’t have any control over distractions in their work environment. I knew I couldn’t reduce the level of their work related stress. But I did everything I could to make their experience seamless and worthwhile.

In this case, I didn't worry about compliance; I worried about the outcome. That's how human-centered design looks like when applied to real problems. That's how essentially technology should be used - to solve problems, help users accomplish their tasks and get the outcome they need.

It is not invented, so that designers, developers and IT people can put themselves on a pedestal and bore and intimidate users with their IT jargon.

ENews

Designed and developed two responsive e-newsletter templates. One newsletter was used to send monthly safety tips to subscribers; another one to send work related updates to employees.

the story workflow

Problem 1

The emergency management agency has the responsibility to educate the public about disaster preparedness and offer easily accessible safety tips. We already had related info stacked up on our Web page, but many complained that it was hard to find our Web page and that we don’t do enough to make it accessible.

In April of 2015, a lot of people affected by the flood signed up for information related to FEMA reimbursement. After I was done with sending flood related information to subscribers, I used the opportunity to offer monthly safety tips. I knew that a monthly newsletter wouldn’t entirely solve our problem, but at least, we were making an effort.

Problem 2

I found out that our e-newsletter platform doesn’t have a mobile responsive template, and most of our subscribers signed up on mobile phones.

Problems Solved

I designed and developed a mobile responsive template and worked with gov.delivery to make adjustments. I was disappointed a little because the end result didn’t work well with all e-mail clients, but I didn’t dwell on it. Back in the day, I created e-newsletters, and I knew about compatibility issues.

I was satisfied with the look and feel and overall functionality, and I knew I met my goal.

Lesson

Do your best, and let go of the rest.

I initiated a monthly e-newsletter with the hope that others would soon contribute with the content, but that didn’t happen. Since I made the initiative, I had to suck it up and come up with new content every month.

Why? I thought it was important.

It is too late to offer safety tips during disasters. It seems irrelevant and cruel to offer safety tips after the disaster strikes, especially to people who are directly affected by it. And during training sessions, people get overwhelmed with too much information.

It sucked that no one really promoted the safety tips, and I never had a chance to do so. I also never had a chance to brainstorm about the content.

Here is one of the UX nuggets. Sometimes, stakeholders won’t tell you what they need. And reviewing tech and business needs won’t help you get to the heart of the issue.

When I created templates, I thought that we were facing tech constraints. In the end, it seemed we were facing staffing and talent issues.

How do you deal with that? How do you deal with situations you have no control over?

WebEOC

Hired to administer emergency management system; ended up re-designing and re-developing the system and taking many roles throughout the project.

the story workflow

BACKGROUND

WebEOC is an online communication system intended for information sharing among 15 emergency groups during disasters. So when all hell breaks loose, everyone involved with emergency operations would log in and learn what is happening on the ground and what is needed.

It was also touted as a platform that would help administrators build customized interfaces on the fly during emergencies. When I took over, it was just a huge mess. It had no purpose and structure. Period.

PROBLEMS

There were too many problems with the system, and I’ll just cite a few of them to shorten the story.

The system had 70 users, most of them sleepers, a few of them active. The system should only be used during big emergencies, but it was still worrisome that so many people didn’t sign up.

Horrible, horrible, horrible interface. I’ll spare you the details. I had never seen anything like it in my entire life. If I do see it in future, I’ll run away pronto But you gather by now, that it was no accident that the system had only few users.

Bad programming. Jumpy interfaces. Unpredictable actions. Unnecessary JavaScript in the background. HTML was old. How old? Who knows? Some well known HTML tags such as links and buttons disabled, and replaced with proprietary tags. Inline CSS used on any page and throughout the system. Some JavaScript functions disabled; replaced with proprietary functions. No documentation and resources for proprietary tags. Too many pages. Too much code. That would all be OK, if the system had any development environment built into it. It didn’t. And I couldn’t copy the code files and build and test in a native environment too. So I had set my self with a nice never ending job

SOLUTION

How did I solve all of that? By following the IT mantra - plan, design, build and test. And by pushing the walls. That’s how I felt most of the time, while trying to work through the issues.

The first hurdle was the hardest. I needed to find out about the structure of the system, its intended use, and users. The fog didn’t clear after reading their documents; they were intended for the outsiders. They didn’t explain about tasks needed to be accomplished during emergencies.

The fog didn’t clear after talking to vendors, IT people, emergency management and a whole bunch of other people.

So I gave the owners of the system two choices: either to stick with what the WebEOC vendor came up, or to customize the system based on their needs. In case they decided to proceed with the second choice - customization - they needed to be specific about their tasks and their needs. They chose to go with the vendor’s version, and agreed it was their job to explain about emergency operations to users.

I decided about the structure and design, and proceeded with building it, page by page. This involved hours and hours of work, cleaning and deciding what to keep, or fix. I swear after so many hours of sifting through the code, I forgot to talk. In any language.

In the end, the system was in a much better shape than before. It had clean, simple, and functional interfaces, and clear purpose.

The system gained new users. The number climbed to about 400 users.

However, the system still relied on a faulty WebEOC framework and that pretty much tied my hands. I was very disappointed with the result of my hard work. Considering the time and effort I put in it, I realized, I could have developed a better and much more useful system on my own, even if I had started with a blank page. And no one does that these days. Everyone tries to speed up through their development phase and automate as many tasks as possible.

Lesson

Don’t try to put a lipstick on a pig. You’re still going to end up with a big, fat, and ugly pig. Put your users first, figure out what they want, and figure out how you’re going to accomplish your goal. In the end, it will all serve a purpose no matter what kind of obstacles you come across.

Also, don’t pay attention to naysayers. Ever. If you try to prove them wrong, you may end up doing what you didn’t want in the first place.

General Electric (2012 - 2013)

Line Art, Illustrations, User Guides, Layout, Design

GE offers really cool black&white guides to its customers, with step-by-step illustrated installation and assembly instructions. I created line art, layout and design for these multi-lingual guides. I also updated wire diagrams occasionally.

Read

Most of my work was deadline driven. Projects were called - Mission 1. Sounds so serious. Right? However, their deadlines were never unreasonable. GE had a really good project pipeline and workflow organization.

What is line art? Think about drawing. When you want to draw an object, you take a pencil or a pen and first draw lines based on your perspective.

In my case, I used Adobe Illustrator and its tool set. I drew washing machines, refrigerators, wires, screws, screwdrivers, hands, and anything in between, in order to help users install and assemble their product in sequence.

It requires concentration to details, and quick and creative thinking. Users don’t care if your illustration is cool or not, they just want to get their boring work done. So if you need to get rid of lines or change a perspective, you better do it, even if you have spent a few hours drawing those lines. Your job is to help them accomplish their task. Pronto.

JFCS (2010 - 2011)

Farmers Market, Micro Enterprise, E-newsletters

JFCS started a farmers market & bazaar to help refugees, enrolled in their micro-enterprise program, overcome their challenges. During the summer, I joined to help with marketing and tech assistance.

Read

The biggest challenge was low visitation, with no budget for promotion. So what did I do? A little bit of everything. If I could have stand on my head, I probably would have.

On Sundays, I would be on the market helping with coordination. During the week, I would come out blazing, handing out flyers, giving presentations, talking to small business owners, community activists, and media. Media with their sorry and stereotypical stories about refugees didn’t help our cause. Except, The Highlander. They really helped by recommending our market to its readers.

What was successful? A weekly e-newsletter. I hand coded the newsletter, stored the template on their drive, and sent the new copies every Friday at noon via Outlook. I would write stories about vendors and include the most important facts about our market. Majority of our subscribers shared the newsletter with their connections.

It was a really neat idea. On the surface, it looked like we were joining a buy local and grow local movement in Louisville. Below the surface, this market was addressing the larger problem. To start a business, you need a credit history and savings. Newcomers to this country had none.

As with any new idea, it takes time to make the inroads. However, non-profits solely shouldn’t bear the burden of initiating programs like this. They have a bigger fish to fry.

If we are serious about community building, then these kind of efforts should be community supported. And they should only be used as a jumping board for refugees, so they can later fly on their own.
