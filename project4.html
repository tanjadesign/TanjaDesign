SMART MEDICAL DEVICES

Health application

IMPORTANT!

I signed the NDA with Care Innovations, so this example is the closest that I can get to a case study. However, it does not represent my usual work of implementing smart medical devices.

Doctors frustrated with ER visits

Our prospective customers, doctors complained that their patients end up in emergency rooms and hospitals even though their conditions are easily manageable. To help patients manage their health better, they were looking to use Health Harmony but found the product too expensive.

Currently, we set up vital sign monitors with iPads and ship everything to new patients. When patients get our devices, everything is configured for them. While this may be an easy to use solution, doctors wanted a more affordable solution.

Possible solution

To reduce the price, we can let patients use Health Harmony with their own devices regardless of size and operating system. They should also be able to hook up and configure their own medical equipmments. To accomplish the goal, we have to re-design the application to fit and work seamlessly with all devices, and to help the patients configure their own equipment easily.

The underlying problem

Doctors are frustrated when their patients end up in emergency rooms and hospitals even though their conditions are easily manageable.

How

Existing User Experience

When reviewing the existing application, I noticed a serious friction between what users need and what interfaces offer. The review revelead a lot of usability issues with messy, cluttered interfaces.

Medical Conditions

I reviewed online medical journals to learn about medical conditions, symptoms and usual treatment plans for each.

Medical professionals

The project lead actually asked me and our iOS developer to join the project. He provided the preliminary research with insights directly provided by medical professionals, who wanted a flexible application, specifically designed for their purposes.

To find out the patients’ perspective, I found a few people with medical conditions and asked them about their expectations.

I watched people with medical conditions try to use the existing interfaces. They could not find what was useful for them when attempting. They liked the automation part of measuring vitals.

Since I already had several new re-designed interfaces, I asked them to use them. All of them preferred the dashboard wireframe that showed visually their health overview and last measuring details.

Competitive overview

I reviewed about 15 apps with similar services; none of them offer the exact service that we do. I included only five with the UX report because I used them all for about a week. The initial look suggested that all of them offer better UX and UI than we do with our own app, but the actual use and detailed overview showed that none of them deliver what they promise to users.

STAGE

Awareness

Dr. Jane Wu just returned from Mt. Sinai Hospital where she was visiting her patient. It was a frustrating visit; the patient had hypoglycemia, a condition he could have easily avoided.

Although limited, Dr. Kingsley’s experience was mostly positive. He said he could monitor patients who were at risk every day and make sure they don’t fall through the cracks.

TOUCH POINT

Web site

EMOTION

Frustration

STAGE

Decision

After talking to the company representative, Ms. Suki and Dr. Wu figured out that the online solution was too expensive, and they were not sure if buying it would really pay off in the long run. Still, they decided to try the solution before committing to it.

TOUCH POINT

Application

They were greeted by a busy and confusing database interface for medical professional users. They were not sure how to complete many tasks, so they relied a lot on a user guide. Using the program, both agreed, was cumbersome.

They were also disappointed with the patient app that was only available on iPad. They assumed that their patients, who already had iPhones and Androids, would be able to download the app on their phones.

EMOTION

Disappointment

They jointly concluded that software won’t fit their current needs. Ms. Suki proceeded with the online search afterward, but couldn’t find anything that would provide the same kind of service.

So Ms. Suki and Dr. Wu agreed to openly tell the company about their current needs. It was not the first time that the company heard about the same kind of demands, so they decided to re-design the product.

Jobs to be done

HIRING THE PRODUCT TO ACCOMPLISH THE GOAL

It would be great if I could see critical information about the state of my patients, so that I can follow their progress over time.

It would be great if I could see when my patients are at health risk, so that I can help them improve their health immediately.

It would be great if I could communicate with my patients immediately when they are at health risk.

It would be great if I could change my patients' treatment quickly when they are at health risk.

It would be great if I could see critical information about the state of my patients, so that I can teach them how to manage their conditions successfully.

Sketching

iOS and Android devices
REACT

Design guidelines

With iOS and Android devices, I had to always produce two design sets for each device because of different default typography, baseline size, different navigation placement, different primary and secondary buttons placements, different set of components and much, much more…

As it turned out, when you build with React, the code is shared among platforms, so the lead developer told me I could only submit one design set and he would use it for both kind of devices.

TWO PHASES

User flows

After we agreed on the scope and limitations, ideation was done in two stages. First, I needed to come up with the user flow for patients entering their vital sign measurements. Then, I proceeded with the user flow for health sessions. I used sketching to capture my frantic thinking.
