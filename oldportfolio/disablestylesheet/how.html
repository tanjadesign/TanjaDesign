CHILDSAFE

DESIGN THINKING

UNDERSTAND

Users, Business Needs, Tech Capacity

During this phase, I collected data about three types of users. It was obvious that there was a valid business need for the online emergency plans. But two questions lingered. Why hasn't it been done before? And how can we accomplish our goals?

PROBLEM 1

According to the law, kindergartens have to submit emergency plans to the emergency management. They would send them by mail, or deliver them in person. Sometimes their plans were handwritten.

But the intent of the law - to help kindergartens better prepare for emergencies, and better account for children’s safety - was not fulfilled.

PROBLEM 2

The local emergency management agency was obliged to accept the plans and store them in an archive. However, they couldn’t use the details when they deploy during emergencies, and having access to these details can make a difference between life and death.

PROBLEM 3

State administrators also had a problem on their hands. In order to issue kindergarten licenses, child care administrators had to check if kindergartens complied with the law. To accomplish that, they had to come to the archive room and sift through emergency plans. This was not their only responsibility. It was one of many.

Findings

The goal during this stage was to find out about user needs, behavior, work environments, and attitudes toward technology.

My previous knowledge helped me with defining challenges of work in kindergartens, call centers, and the emergency management environment. I had to rely on first responder experts to find out about procedures, scenes and organization in their work environments. Interviews filled the gaps with everything else.

Competitive overview showed that no agency, state or local, was working on anything similar.

Our biggest obstacle was that we didn't have a development team on board, or development tools on disposal, and we needed to keep up with increased security and data protection guidelines.

DEFINE

Design Strategy and Focus

After I found the way to overcome our biggest obstacle, I started pouring all my user research into use cases, personas, jobs-to-be-done, and user journeys. My personas were goal oriented, but I accomplished a whole lot more with jobs-to-be-done.

Limitations

Our biggest obstacle was that we didn't have a development team on board, or development tools on disposal, so the big portion of my time was spent on finding suitable development tools that would offer a secure storage and data encryption.

Funding and a so called prototype

When I found the right solution, I signed up for a trial period. I managed to check the main capabilities, and then I set out to get the funding.

Instead of scheduling a meeting with all the people who decided about our budget, I managed to get a key support from one person, who checked my basic prototype and gave her input. She then won the support of other people, and I got the funding to proceed. My solution really saved the money and resources.

The first prototype included several screens that I printed out, according to the users' journeys. This pre-prototype was just used for this purpose, and kept as a reference. I didn't build upon it.

Persona

HAZARDS SPECIALIST

Joshua Vanderhof

Joshua cares about his job, but he is greeted by the red tape wherever he turns.

“Look at this,” he says, while holding an emergency plan in his hand. “Can you read anything on it? Neither can I!”

PROBLEMS

In this particular case, a kindergarten employee left an emergency plan at the reception. It was handwritten. Now Joshua needs to find their telephone number or an email address to let them know that the agency has the plan. When they ask for feedback, he can't give them any.

Joshua is buried with paperwork. He is on the tight deadlines with several grant proposals. In addition, he is working hard to update the new hazards mitigation plan. He attends the city-wide meetings, exercises, and evaluations. He collaborates with all the players - consultants, the representatives of city agencies, non-profits, the local university, etc.

In addition, he is not in the best shape of his life. After his heart bypass surgery, he needs to keep his stress level low, and adhere to strict diet guidelines.

“Man, I don’t have time for this,” he says, while walking hurriedly to the archive room to place the emergency plan in its appropriate place.

On the way there, he meets another employee, Karen and says: “Look what I got.”

“Another one,” Karen says. “But we can’t use any of it.”

Nothing in the plan is data sensitive, but everything can be used for operational services during disasters. It can help with search and rescue. It can help with emergency planning. It can help with mitigation.

SOLUTION

Create a responsive online application for emergency plans. It should be accessible from all mobile devices and modern browsers.

“That would be wonderful,” says Joshua. “Don’t talk to me about Chrome or Firefox. I’ll do whatever you tell me to do. But it should be easy to use. OK?”

Persona

KINDERGARTEN TEACHER

Abbey Abramov

Abbey quit her previous job at an IT company because she just couldn’t stand the toxic work environment anymore. For as long as she knows, Abbey enjoyed being around children and taking care of them.

PROBLEMS

So it was no-brainer that she decided to open a kindergarten.

However, she now has doubts about her plans.

“There are so many regulations,” says Abbey. “I’m not sure if I can keep up with all of them.”

Abbey completed several training sessions with ChildCare Aware, and now she has to concentrate on emergency planning.

“I don’t know how to create an emergency plan,” says Abbey. “I don’t know what goes into it. What if I make a mistake.”

SOLUTION

Include an emergency plan example with the online application. New kindergarten users will be able to save the copy on their drive, or just log in and study the plan.

“Thank you,” says Abbey. “Thank you so much for doing this.”

In addition, include step by step guidance to help all kindergarten users create emergency plans.

“So, can I create a plan before I get the license from the Division of Child Care,” asks Abbey.

Sure.

Persona

Lea James

After a long day at work, Lea settles on the couch and turns on the TV to catch up with the news.

“Shooting rampage at Madison High School left four students injured today,” said broadcaster.

“That’s horrible,” Lea blurts out. “Poor kids.”

PROBLEMS

A sense of dread suddenly becomes overwhelming. Ooh dear god, she thought to herself. I forgot to send our emergency plan.

Every year, Lea would obsess about deadlines for emergency plans. She would also obsess about sharing updated emergency plans with the parents.

To make even a minor change, Lea has to open a Word doc, and save the updates as a pdf doc.

She then makes additional copies for all staff members. She than hands one copy to each parent at the end of the day. In addition, she sends one copy to the emergency management via mail.

Lea doesn’t mind complying with the law. It’s there to keep her children and staff safe. However, she finds the entire process cumbersome. She often wonders if anyone reads emergency plans.

The more she learns about emergency planning, the more she worries. What if we can’t call 911 during emergencies? How will anyone know that we need help?

What if something happens in our neighborhood? Who will alert us?

She is especially worried this year. During one of the outside activities, children and staff saw a man bleeding profusely from the gunshot wound. After the event, all of them were traumatized.

SOLUTION

Create a responsive online application for emergency plans. It should be accessible from all mobile devices and modern browsers.

“That’s a wonderful idea. Just wonderful,” says Ms. Marple from ChildCare Aware. “When do you think you can get it done? Of course, we’ll help in any way we can.”

VALIDATE

User Testing, Stakeholder Validations, Tech Capacity

First, we needed the approval of our major stakeholders - ChildCare Aware, Division of ChildCare, and our director. Then, we could proceed with user testing and user surveys. The most fun part during this stage was watching kindergarten teachers use the application, while uninterrupted. I knew I made it. Yupiiii

Success

Since I found the way to avoid our common tech pitfalls, the usability testing (functionalities) was successful from the get go. I still had to deal with a negative cloud and positive cloud judgment. User onboarding and demos helped with removing the negative cloud judgment. Verbal feedback from the users was overall positive, but that didn’t convince me that I succeeded. Observations helped me assess that. While watching users navigate the application and across the interfaces, I realized that their level of understanding was complete.
