Fundraising project

Cancer fitness program

Livestrong at the YMCA helps cancer patients who suffer from the disease, chemotherapy and other treatments.

When they join, a personal trainer first meets with the patients to assess their overall health and their primary goals. Then, the trainer develops a fitness program for each person. Their membership is free for 12 weeks.

Swimming program for unpriviledged kids

Safety around Water program teaches unprivileged children how to survive in water for free. When they join, a swimming instructor determines their swimming levels, and then develop a program that would help them learn survival skills no matter how they end up in water.

This year, the Southeast facility taught survival water skills to 1076 children. The facility offers these essential classes to all children in the West End for free.

Fundraising difficulties

Free programs like this depend from individual donations, and the YMCA as a whole doesn’t share its donations. Each facility has to conduct a separate fundraising campaign. The Southeast YMCA facility has a problem because they operate in a diverse, but lower income Buechel neighborhood. That’s why this branch has to develop a new and successful fundraising strategy.

The underlying problem

The latest research revealed one more problem for YMCA Southeast. Donors like to contribute to multiple organizations but demand for greater transparency and accountability. That wouldn’t be a problem if people knew about the YMCA free programs and their benefits to the community. But it turns out, they don’t.

How

I reviewed the national Livestrong Web site to learn about their mission, communication style, branding, typography, and visual assets.

I reviewed the existing Donations print sheet to learn about the obstacles users and fundraiser face with in-person donations.

I started the heurestic analysis of the YMCA Southeast Web site and its donations online page but didn’t see the need to proceed because going over shortcomings with the donation workflow was tiring. The usability issues were abundant and obvious.

Team

I conducted in-depth, in-person interviews with the Livestrong coordinator and the director of the swimming program several times.

I wanted to understand underlying motivations behind their involvement; to learn about particular tasks involved when enrolling cancer patients and kids into their free programs; and to learn about obstacles they and their members face during duration of programs and afterward.

Livestrong members

I interviewed Livestrong members and their family in-person and via online tools. The goal was not just to find out about the actual benefits of Livestrong, but to understand how the program fits with their challenging lifestyles.

My unique background put me in a privileged position in this case. They opened up about private and sensitive issues, their vulnerabilities and how society treats people with chronic and terminal illnesses. They also provided a lot of input how the program could expand and improve in the long run.

Donors

Surveys with diverse participants revealed one theme. People donate to their favorite causes. To succeed, organizations and programs have to find their niche audience based on the cause. To appeal to people who never considered them before, they have to go the extra mile.

Medical professionals

Unfortunately, I had to give up from this survey. The hospitals's IT security blocked my surveys and nurses could not complete them, although we tried several times. I was sad because nurses always have the valuable, practical advice for patients. That's why I wanted to find out about their perspective and advice when engaging cancer patients with exercise.

I observed silently while exercising in the gym. During observations, it is imperative that you engage without any judgment or expectations.

Curiously, I watched how Livestrong coordinator was bouncing from the Livestrong sessions to the YMCA team meetings and other classes she taught.

I paid attention to the facial expressions of Livestrong members while they exercised. I paid attention to their physical demeanour and the level of strain they managed to endure.

Regular YMCA members didn’t have a clue they were exercising next to the people fighting for their lives.

Competitive overview

I haven’t found any free swimming or water safety classes, especially not for the children of West End.

However, I found a lot of free classes offered to cancer patients. They ranged in their offerings. Some of them treated cancer patients as preschoolers; some of them overestimated their physical abilities during exhausting treatments; and some of them were patronizing and certainly not empowering. As far as I could see, none of them were based on the scientific research.

How do you decide if any of them compare to Livestrong at the YMCA? What could be the metrics for comparisons?

Luckily, in this case we don’t have think about it. The surveys have already shown that donors donate to their favorite causes. We are going to assume that we compete with many local non-profits because we compete for the same amount of donations money.

Usability guidelines for landing pages

A landing page is independent of the main Web site. It's created specifically to receive the traffic for a campaign. Here are usability guidelines for effective landing pages:

  • Don't include navigation or other distractions.
  • Appeal to emotions.
  • Design for one purpose only - explain who you are, what you do, and purpose of your page.
  • Include clear compelling headline to capture the interest.
  • Include images and videos that are connected to your message.
  • Write content that will keep them energized and interested, and avoid jargon.
  • Feature customer testimonials highlighting the benefits of your service.

Usability guidelines for donation pages

According to the UX experts, non-profits act as if they don't care about online donations. Poor design decisions cause usability problems and users' turn-off.

Many spend a considerable amount of time looking for information, and leave non-profit Web sites unable to finish the donation process.

User research finds significant deficiencies in non-profit organizations' website content, which often fails to provide the info people need to make donation decisions.

Nielsen Norman Group

The Nielsen Norman group has already conducted extensive studies that may help many designers avoid users' turn-off. Here is what they recommend as usability guidelines:

  • Clearly explain what the organization does and how the work is done, in a succinct and easy to understand manner.
  • Appeal to emotions.
  • Disclose how donations are used. Don't hide this information from users.
  • Display third party endorsements to prove credibility - watchdog ratings, high profile endorsements, testimonials, name recognition, and number of years in operation. Encourage your supporters to recommend the organization to family and friends - social proof principle.
  • Provide a noticeable and clear link to donate. When asking for monetary donations, the phrases Donate and Donate Now are immediately understood by the users.
  • Streamline the donation process. Make it fast. Mimic the e-commerce checkout process. Don't require extra steps - more typing or decision making. The less time users spend with the donation process, the better likelihood they will finish the process.

LIVESTRONG COORDINATOR

Stacey Pollack

Stacey Pollack loves her job, even though she works in a hectic environment. Her attention shifts from the gym members and equipment malfunctions to the personal trainers she supervises and the fundraising campaign.

She knows that her job is important.

“We have nothing, if we don’t have health,” Stacey said.

That's why she runs the program for cancer patients - Livestrong at the YMCA.

MAKING DIFFERENCE

Building strength

When her stepfather Charlie was diagnosed with brain cancer, she felt helpless. Her stepfather died from the disease, which left her heartbroken.

That’s why she now takes time to help cancer patients who suffer from the disease, chemotherapy and other treatments.

She develops fitness programs for them, so they can build strength and battle fatigue after exhaustive treatments.

FITNESS PROGRAM

Personal goals

When they join, she first meets with them to assess their overall health and their primary goals. Then, she develops a personal training program for each person.

Their membership is free for 12 weeks. Most of them take advantage of the 12 weeks, but a quarter of them never finish due to health complications and job restrictions.

FUNDRAISING

Need new strategy

Free programs like this depend from individual donations, and the YMCA as a whole doesn’t share its donations. Each facility has to conduct a separate fundraising campaign. But there is a problem that this Southeast YMCA facility faces. They operate in a diverse, but lower income Buechel neighborhood, mostly populated by immigrants.

"Not a millionaire neighborhood like Brownsboro," says Stacey.

That’s why this branch has to step up with its fundraising game this year.

When she asked for financial assistance from local non-profits, none of them helped her. And their response, it seems, left deep scars. You won’t see her advocating for cancer related causes or local non-profits. But she agreed to support Livestrong at the YMCA Southeast, if needed.

LIVESTRONG

Impressions

Livestrong doesn’t turn away any cancer patients from joining. Patients only need a doctor’s approval.

Lydia never goes online, and she doesn’t intend to. She didn’t complain about the long paper form she had to fill before joining Livestrong.

Testing the hypothesis

According to one stakeholder, the organization cannot have the successful fundraising because they operate in a low income immigrant neighborhood.

“Not a millionaire neighborhood like Brownsboro.”

But according to the social proof theory, properly conveyed messages can encourage many people to join movements or support social causes and missions. When people don’t know much about the cause, they are looking to similar people like them for clues whether to join or not.

So I hastily organized fundraising with the help of a few women. Interested in outcome?

Sketching

Large, medium and small devices
FIRST VERSION

Large and medium devices

On a large device and a tablet landscape display, the hero area is divided in two - Livestrong and Safety around Water.

Both sections are competing with each other; there is no visual hierarchy. It also appears cluttered already. Imagine if we add one more button to each section; you would have 4 buttons demanding some kind of an action.

FIRST VERSION

Small devices

On small devices like phones, the section on the right would appear less important because it would go underneath the left section. That’s not what we are trying to convey. We have one facility with two different free programs; both with an important purpose.

We don’t’ want to make a decision instead of our users. We want them to find out about both programs and decide if they would donate to one or the other, or split their donation between the two programs.

Let's keep going and see where it takes us.

SECOND VERSION

Large and medium devices

On a large device and a tablet landscape, the hero section contains the text area on the left and visual area on the right - containing ribbons (cancer fight symbols) and a splash of water. Both programs are incorporated. Call for action serves users who already know about the free programs and want to donate right away. Learn more serves users who want to find out more about the programs before investing their donation money.

Then we continue with WHY we exist - our members: cancer patients and underprivileged children. We also included our accomplishments before the call for action again.

SECOND VERSION

Small devices

On small mobile devices, we include the text area first, followed by the visuals on the second screen. Just like on large devices, we explain WHY we exist, and what we accomplished. Then we call for donations again.

This idea turned out to be exactly what we need. Let's keep going.

Prototype

The landing page was developed for prospective donors. It explains the accomplishments of Southeast Family YMCA and many benefits that their free programs bring to the residents of Louisville.

This landing page can also be used by fundraisers during the annual YMCA campaign.

Visit

Why was this solution successful?

Potential donors

These type of users are greeted with the visuals and clear explanation about what these two programs do and how. They can go further to learn more about each program through videos, onboarding steps, and accomplishments.

In addition, they can learn how members, their family and employees feel about these programs. The call for action to donate is very prominent and strategically placed in two places. One is for the users who already know about the programs and want to immediately donate. Another one is placed after persuasive and emotional testimonials.

The entire landing page has a coherent and interesting content that appeals to emotions, but doesn’t pull at the heartstrings.

Fundraisers

Fundraising folks can use the entire page for the presentation either to a large crowd, or individuals, and then ask people to donate immediately. They can also share the link through social media, email or text messages. This page is basically their amplifier, and their tool.

Challenges

IT service

The challenge came up when we needed the IT department to provide the hosting space and connect our new donation form to the specific fundraising for free programs at the Southeast YMCA.

Everybody tried to reason with the head of IT, who was basically a marketing professional. Because of his lack of IT knowledge, he didn’t even know what I asked for.

I also witnessed when the director of the swimming program and the YMCA executive director talked to the IT guy. He basically refused to do anything about accommodating our efforts.

The problem with the Louisville YMCA is that the general leadership, with their centralized bureaucracy, prevents the struggling branches from making any strides with membership and fundraising, but don’t offer the shared fund to all branches. Usually, when you set the rules, you are the one that is financing the efforts. That’s not the case with this organization.

While we managed to solve the hosting space ourselves, we never got our donation form published. So effectively, the IT department prevented the entire branch from fundraising specifically for free programs.

What did I learn?

Some people come to your life for a reason and a season.

I believe that the YMCA people from my neighborhood branch came into my life, so I could restore my faith in the humanity again. They are a great bunch. Genuinely kind, open to new experiences and diversity prone.

While our project altogether didn’t turn out the way we wanted to, I’m still one phone call away, if they need any help. IF we EVER get out of this pandemic.
