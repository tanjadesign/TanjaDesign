YMCA: LIVESTRONG AND SAFETY AROUND WATER

DESIGN THINKING

UNDERSTAND

Users, Business Needs, Tech Capacity

I interviewed the team members, Livestrong members and their family. I completed a short heuristic analysis of existing Web sites after reviewing guidelines for landing and donation pages. I also observed a lot. Take a look at what I found out.

PROBLEM 1

Livestrong at the YMCA helps cancer patients who suffer from the disease, chemotherapy and other treatments.

When they join, a personal trainer first meets with the patients to assess their overall health and their primary goals. Then, the trainer develops a fitness program for each person. Their membership is free for 12 weeks.

Free programs like this depend from individual donations, and the YMCA as a whole doesn’t share its donations. Each facility has to conduct a separate fundraising campaign. The Southeast YMCA facility has a problem because they operate in a diverse, but lower income Buechel neighborhood. That’s why this branch has to develop a new and successful fundraising strategy.

PROBLEM 2

Safety around Water program teaches unprivileged children how to survive in water for free. When they join, a swimming instructor determines their swimming levels, and then develop a program that would help them learn survival skills no matter how they end up in water.

This year, the Southeast facility taught survival water skills to 1000 children. The facility offers these essential classes to all children in the West End for free.

Just like Livestrong, Safety around Water also depends from individual donations. The latest research revealed one more problem for YMCA Southeast. Donors like to contribute to multiple organizations but demand for greater transparency and accountability. That wouldn’t be a problem if people knew about the YMCA free programs and their benefits to the community. But it turns out, they don’t.

TECH CAPACITY

Our volunteer will design and develop a responsive landing page and then implement the CMS with the page. We don't anticipate any unusual difficulties with the technical work.

She will also come up with a newsletter template for fundraising and start a Facebook page for Livestrong members.

COMPETITIVE OVERVIEW

I kind of got stuck with this one. I haven’t found any free swimming or water safety classes , especially not for the children of West End. However, I found a lot of free classes offered to cancer patients.

How do you decide if any of them compare to Livestrong at the YMCA? So for now, we’re going to stop with competitive analysis. We are going to assume that we compete with many local non-profits even though they don’t offer the same services. However, we compete for the same amount of donations money.

Persona

HEALTHY LIVING COORDINATOR

Stacey Pollack

Stacey Pollack loves her job, even though she works in a hectic environment. Her attention shifts from the gym members and equipment malfunctions to the personal trainers she supervises and the fundraising campaign.

She knows that her job is important.

“We have nothing, if we don’t have health,” Stacey said.

PROBLEM

There is one program that holds the special meaning for Stacey - Livestrong at the YMCA.

When her stepfather Charlie was diagnosed with brain cancer, she felt helpless. Her stepfather died from the disease, which left her heartbroken.

That’s why she now takes time to help cancer patients who suffer from the disease, chemotherapy and other treatments. After 25 years as a personal trainer and physical therapy education, this is how she can contribute the most - motivate cancer patients and teach them how to build strength and battle fatigue.

And she noticed the difference she makes. The Livestrong members walked a little faster; smiled more and appeared more confident, she said.

When they join, she first meets with them to assess their overall health and their primary goals. Then, she develops a personal training program for each person. Their membership is free for 12 weeks. Most of them take advantage of the 12 weeks, but a quarter of them never finish due to health complications and job restrictions.

Free programs like this depend from individual donations, and the YMCA as a whole doesn’t share its donations. Each facility has to conduct a separate fundraising campaign. But there is a problem that this Southeast YMCA facility faces. They operate in a diverse, but lower income Buechel neighborhood, mostly populated by immigrants. Not a millionaire neighborhood like Brownsboro, says Stacey. That’s why this branch has to step up with its fundraising game this year.

SOLUTION

Create a set of online material and engage in offline promotional activities in the community:

  • Landing page
  • E-Newsletter
  • Facebook page
  • Engage volunteers for community events

Assess your impact and figure out how to motivate former members, and maintain the communication with them.

GOALS

  • Improve the physical strength of current Livestrong members
  • Improve their overall health
  • Motivate the Livestrong members to continue with exercise after their 12 week free of charge period
  • Attract more cancer patients to the program
  • Improve fundraising for the program

Persona

LIVESTRONG MEMBER

Lydia Glogov

It’s been two months, since Lydia Glogov joined Livestrong at the YMCA. And she wasn’t a happy joiner, she said, although she was weak and exhausted all the time. But she relented under the pressure from her daughter and her doctor.

She already sleeps better, she said. She doesn’t wake up 20 times at night, as she did before.

Lydia is remission happy after her breast cancer treatment, but her journey has not been easy.

“Financially, it killed me,” she said.

PROBLEM

When she asked for financial assistance from local non-profits, none of them helped her. And their response, it seems, left deep scars. You won’t see her advocating for cancer related causes or local non-profits. But she agreed to support Livestrong at the YMCA Southeast, if needed.

Before joining Livestrong, she never exercised, but she swears now she would continue even after the free 12 week period. And she’d like to stay in touch with the instructor and also meet other Livestrong members.

Livestrong doesn’t turn away any cancer patients from joining. Patients only need a doctor’s approval.

Lydia never goes online, and she doesn’t intend to. She didn’t complain about the long paper form she had to fill before joining Livestrong.

SOLUTION

Create a print newsletter with tips for healthy living and the news about latest Livestrong developments.

  • Improve the overall health
  • Stay in touch with the program and other members
  • Start believing in humanity again

Persona

AQUATICS DIRECTOR

Shirley Davies

Shirley Davies knows that her job is important.

“Drowning is the second leading cause of death for children ages 1 to 14,” she is quick to point out.

She joined the YMCA as a 12-year-old. She likes to say that she was a terrible swimmer until the age 26 when she became a lifeguard.

Shirley lives and breathes the YMCA spirit where no one is turned away because they can’t pay.

“We offer free swimming lessons to all children in the West End, ” she said. “Watching them go from water scared to swimming like a fish is amazing.”

PROBLEM

During the four weeks in 2018, 1,000 children participated in the free Safety around Water classes at two sites - Central and Shawnee High School.

Also, Livestrong helped 34 cancer patients in 2018; some of them used the pool for cardio classes.

Shirley hopes that the February campaign will help them expand and improve quality of the programs. She hopes to hire more instructors and have enough time to be more involved in the community. Eventually, she would like to have safety swimming classes available during the entire year.

This year, they are expected to raise $105,000. Based on the latest research, it won’t be easy. About 90 percent of donors donate to multiple organizations and they want to know where their money is going. Most of them are not familiar with the benefits of YMCA and its programs.

SOLUTION

Create a landing page that explains WHY the YMCA and its free programs exist.

Train volunteers to share the same story with potential donors and the community.

IDEATE

Explore Many Options

I had a few layout ideas, and you can see some of the sketches here. I believe that the second idea can lead to our destination.

Scope

At this point, we are not sure about the scope. We thought we were going to create a landing page and an e-newsletter, but we may also have to add a print newsletter, or maybe form a new group to satisfy the need of our users.

Some may call this a scope creep, but our goal all along was to improve fundraising, not just to come up with a new Web site or some other marketing material.

Some may be confused with how is fundraising connected with expanding or improving programs, but if you follow along, the connection between the two will be clear in the end.

Sketching

1.The first idea didn't turn out good.

On a large device and a tablet landscape, the hero area is divided in two - Livestrong and Safety around Water.

Both sections are competing with each other; there is no visual hierarchy. It also appears cluttered already. Imagine if we add one more button to each section; you would have 4 buttons demanding some kind of an action.

On small devices like phones, the section on the right would appear less important because it would go underneath the left section. That’s not what we are trying to convey. We have one facility with two different free programs; both with an important purpose.

We don’t’ want to make a decision instead of our users. We want them to find out about both programs and decide if they would donate to one or the other, or split their donation between the two programs.

Let's keep going and see where it takes us.


2. The second idea is better, but only if we can come up with a compelling call for action. Many companies don’t dedicate a copywriter to a team, which in my opinion is a big mistake. Some of us may be good with words, but we are still biased because we already invested our work in the new product.

On a large device and a tablet landscape, the hero section contains the text area on the left and visual area on the right - containing ribbons (cancer fight symbols) and a splash of water. Both programs are incorporated. Call for action serves users who already know about the free programs and want to donate right away. Learn more serves users who want to find out more about the programs before investing their donation money.

Then we continue with WHY we exist - our members: cancer patients and underprivileged children. We also included our accomplishments before the call for action again.

On small mobile devices, we include the text area first, followed by the visuals on the second screen. Just like on large devices, we explain WHY we exist, and what we accomplished. Then we call for donations again.
