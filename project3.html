CHILDSAFE

Emergency application

Kindergartens unprepared for emergencies

According to the law, kindergartens have to submit emergency plans to the emergency management. They would send them by mail, or deliver them in person. Sometimes their plans were handwritten.

But the intent of the law - to help kindergartens better prepare for emergencies, better account for children’s safety, and better communicate with the local emergency management agency - was not fulfilled.

Emergency agency frustrated by a lack of emergency data

The local emergency agency was obligated to accept the plans and store them in an archive. However, they couldn’t use the details when they deploy during emergencies, and having access to these details can make a difference between life and death.

Their frustration was also tied to their inability to purse their larger strategic goal. During disasters, the agency is supposed to help with coordinating efforts on the ground and that includes providing accurate information to first responders.

For instance, if we have an emergency in the zip codes 40205 and 40207, knowing emergency details would help with deciding what kind of resources are needed on the ground - ambulances, fire trucks, people, etc. It could also help with planning for future emergencies.

The state law compliance check required on-site arrival

State administrators also had a problem on their hands. In order to issue kindergarten licenses, child care administrators had to check if kindergartens complied with the law. To accomplish that, they had to come to the archive room and sift through emergency plans. This was not their only responsibility. It was one of many.

The underlying problem

Kiddos in Louisville's kindergartens are especially vulnerable during emergencies because emergency staff don't have the right data at the right time.

BACKGROUND

How did everything come about?

My coworker summoned the deputy director in his office to discuss child care centers. All I knew at the time was that he was asking her to find someone that would call kindergartens. She resisted, but then asked me to integrate a solution with our emergency communication system. She explained that we needed interfaces for the contact data of Louisville kindergartens.

I said that I either needed people to input the data, or have the data in Excell or .cvs format.

THEY SAID THEY DIDN’T HAVE THE DATA.

How in the heck can you call kindergartens, if you don’t have their contact info?

It’s the law, my co-worker said, we have to do it. Needless to say, I was confused.

Contraversy continued

After that initial conversation, there was always some contraversy with emergency plans. People would puff, moan, and complain about emergency plans, and that they needed to place them in our archive room. I didn’t know at the time that kindergartens’ contacts and emergency plans were connected.

Breakthrough

One day, I started talking with one of my coworkers, who was holding the emergency plan in his hand. I asked him if I could look at the plan. As soon as I laid my eyes on it, I knew I was looking at the gold.

“Do you know that this is a very valuable information for us,” I asked.

Of course, he did. He explained what was happening with emergency plans and how valuable information is rotting in the archive room, while we desperately need it during emergencies. Now, this was a way to communicate.

Saving lives and preventing injuries is what fueled me. I said I would find a solution to the problem, and I started quietly working on preparations.

How

I reached out to our emergency plan coordinator to join the project and to setup a meeting with ChildCare Aware - the agency that supervises and provides training to all kindergartens in the Louisville area.

During the meeting with the agency, I explained about the project and asked them to join us and facilitate our implementation. They wholeheartedly agreed and I couldn’t be happier.

Existing docs

Since there was no application before, we had to start from the paper emergency plans. When I reviewed the plans, I noticed that kindergartens didn’t use any template. Some of them even submitted handwritten plans.

When I reached out to the ChildCare Aware, it turned out that the paper template was available to download on their Web site.

So I started from the template that included a lot of optional questions that made the plan really long. If I had included every question from the template in our initial online application, it would have been a huge usability issue.

State law

The state law specifically underlined the intent and purpose of emergency plans for kindergartens. When I analyzed it, I figured out that maybe we can work on initially shortening the template plan, but when the plan gets adapted by the kindergartens, include more questions that are useful to all stakeholders.

FEMA guidelines

To learn about the best practices for emergency plans, I reviewed emergency plan guidelines for kindergartens and schools developed by FEMA.

Outcome

I reached out to ChildCare Aware to see if they object to changing their template for the online application, and to reveal what I had in mind for the future.

I also asked them to reach out to the Division of Child Care and the lawmakers to see if they would agree to changing the template, but also to make sure that we are not overstepping our boundary. We needed their approval to proceed.

Emergency management

I structured my interview so I can interview the entire group at the same time. I didn’t want to give them a chance to air their demands because from my experience, I knew it would lead to nowhere. I already knew about their needs, but wanted to find out how they operated when the push comes to show. Based on the interview, I laid out the goals for the emergency dashboard application.

First responders

Firefighters are usually first on the scene when the type of emergency is not known to dispatchers. So how would they find out emergency details in case of a kindergarten emergency?

For that reason, I interviewed an expert who previously led a division of firefighters. This interview was very useful for assessing how first responders operate; what obstacles they usually encounter in different environments; and what kind level of stress they endure.

All other stakeholders

Interviewing coupled with observation was done throughout the entire lifecycle of the project. Interviewing followed by usability or user testing was also done throughout the entire lifecycle of the project.

Observations, both environmental and contextual, were the only methods I used for this purpose.

I used surveys for quick user testing when I couldn’t handle the volume of users; when I couldn’t be in the room with them to do a moderated testing; and when I couldn't do online unmoderated testing.

Competitive overview

Because I didn’t have any resources, I approached to the so called Digital Team thinking they would be interested in taking on the project. They didn’t want any part in it. Saving kiddos from the fire, or school shooting wasn’t sexy enough for them. I’m not sarcastic here, just realistic.

It turned out that a state government employee attempted to move emergency plans online, but that attempt would just let users upload emergency plans like attachments.

My further research showed that no agency, state or local, was working on anything similar to our project. So we were starting from scratch.

Challenges

Our biggest obstacle was that we didn't have a development team on board, or development tools on disposal, so the big portion of my time was spent on finding suitable development tools that would offer a secure storage and data encryption.

When I found the right solution, I signed up for a trial period. I managed to check the main capabilities, and then I set out to get the funding.

The first prototype included several screens that I printed out, according to the users' journeys. This pre-prototype was just used for this purpose, and kept as a reference. I didn't build upon it, but used it to brainstorm with the person I asked for help. This person saw the value in what I was doing and was worried about the safety issues with kindergartens, schools and daycare centers. Her name is Angie Down.

Angie then won the support of other people, and I got the funding to proceed. In the end, my solution saved the money and resources. At that moment, I knew that this project would succeed and I knew where I was going.

Persona I

BACKGROUND, BEHAVIOR, PSYCHOLOGY...
Hazards Specialist

Joshua Vanderhof

Joshua cares about his job, but he is greeted by the red tape wherever he turns.

“Look at this,” he says, while holding an emergency plan in his hand. “Can you read anything on it? Neither can I!”

In this particular case, a kindergarten employee left an emergency plan at the reception. It was handwritten. Now Joshua needs to find their telephone number or an email address to let them know that the agency has the plan.

When they ask for feedback, he can't give them any.

PSYCHOLOGY

Red tape, deadlines

Joshua is buried with paperwork. He is on the tight deadlines with several grant proposals.

In addition, he is working hard to update the new hazards mitigation plan. He attends the city-wide meetings, exercises, and evaluations. He collaborates with all the players - consultants, the representatives of city agencies, non-profits, the local university, etc.

CONSEQUENCES

Heart disease

Joshua is not in the best shape of his life. After his heart bypass surgery, he needs to keep his stress level low, and adhere to strict diet guidelines.

“Man, why everything around here has to be so complicated,” he says, while walking hurriedly to the archive room to place the emergency plan in its appropriate place.

CONFRONTING THE ISSUE

If you don't use it, you lose it

On the way to the archive room, he meets another employee, Karen and says: “Look what I got.”

“Another one,” Karen says. “But we can’t use any of it.”

Nothing in the plan is data sensitive, but everything can be used for operational services during disasters. It can help with search and rescue. It can help with emergency planning. It can help with mitigation.

It would be great to know how many kids are in the kindergarten in case of an emergency, so we can easily assess if anyone is missing after the rescue.

It would be great to have an outline of the floor plan in case of an emergency, so we can determine where their shelter and exits are.

It would be great to know how many kids have physical impairments in case of an emergency, so we can determine what special equipment we need for the rescue.

It would be great to know how many kids have chronic health issues, so we can have appropriate medical staff nearby.

It would be great if we could successfully communicate with the teachers in case of an emergency, so we can easily coordinate emergency operations.

Persona II

BACKGROUND, BEHAVIOR, PSYCHOLOGY...
KINDERGARTEN ADMIN

Lea James

After a long day at work, Lea settles on the couch and turns on the TV to catch up with the news.

“Shooting rampage at Madison High School left four students injured today,” said broadcaster.

“That’s horrible,” Lea blurts out. “Poor kids.”

PSYCHOLOGY

Deadlines, children safety, parents

A sense of dread suddenly becomes overwhelming. Ooh dear god, she thought to herself. I forgot to send our emergency plan.

Every year, Lea would obsess about deadlines for emergency plans. She would also obsess about sharing updated emergency plans with the parents.

EMERGENCY PLAN UPDATE

Cumbersome manual process

To make even a minor change, Lea has to open a Word doc, and save the updates as a pdf doc.

She then makes additional copies for all staff members. She than hands one copy to each parent at the end of the day. In addition, she sends one copy to the emergency management via mail.

Lea doesn’t mind complying with the law. It’s there to keep her children and staff safe. However, she finds the entire process cumbersome. She often wonders if anyone reads emergency plans.

PSYCHOLOGY

Are we really safe?

The more Lea learns about emergency planning, the more she worries. What if we can’t call 911 during emergencies? How will anyone know that we need help?

What if something happens in our neighborhood? Who will alert us?

She is especially worried this year. During one of the outside activities, children and staff saw a man bleeding profusely from the gunshot wound. After the event, all of them were traumatized.

Jobs to be done

HIRING THE PRODUCT TO ACCOMPLISH THE GOAL

It would be great, if I could update emergency plans quickly without manual process of re-creating, so I could spend less time doing unnecessary repetitive work.

It would be great, if I could share the updated emergency plans immediately with all the stakeholders.

It would be great, if we could communicate timely with emergency services and first responders.

It would be great, if we could prepare better for different kind of emergencies, so we can react more effectively when the various emergencies occur.

It would be great, if we could help children and staff cope better and develop resiliency when faced with unfortunate events in their community.

Why was this solution successful?

Emergency management

Before, emergency staff could only react blindly and do as best as they can during operations. With this solution, they maximize their chances when it comes to saving lives and preventing injuries. They don’t just have the contact info, but can pull specific information related to each emergency, or a large disaster when you have several emergencies at the same time.

They can better prepare before each emergency. They can safely and efficiently reunite parents or guardians with their children after emergencies. They can identify weaknesses and make a mitigation plan based on that.

They can communicate with kindergartens daily, and provide preparedness training on the site. They can see the big picture and the small details. They can access this information from any device at the right time.

Kindergartens

Kindergartens and preschools can now easily create and make changes to their emergency plans whenever they want. They are not tied to any place, time, or a particular device. Think about their work environment for a bit. Do you think that the owners of small kindergartens can concentrate, while kids are crying or throwing tantrums around them? Environment is an important factor in this case.

State of mind is important too. Kindergarten staff don’t feel alone, confused and frightened when thinking about emergencies.

They don’t have to worry about deadlines anymore. Their emergency plan is now the living document. They now understand that updating their emergency plan is important for their safety.

They can show initiative and reach out to emergency staff for preparedness sessions and training. Confidence comes from preparation, and fear dissipates when your action is based on knowledge.

State administration

State administrators don’t have to come to the dusty and crowded archive room to check if kindergartens comply with the law. They don’t have to decipher illegible documents. They don’t have to remind kindergartens about deadlines. They don’t have to listen to these emergency folks always complaining about the absurdity of emergency plans implementation. They can outsource emergency preparedness training to the agency with more practical knowledge about it. They can view, edit and delete at their will.

Beyond the project

When Angie and I talked, we knew that this concept could apply to public schools also. I was getting ready to continue in that direction before implementing the special dashboard for emergency management.

ChildCare Aware already notified daycare centers for seniors and people with disabilities to start using ChildSafe. I was getting ready to modify the application for their purposes.

This concept can really be implemented for any public facility in Louisville. The network administrator Dana and I talked about the wider application. I could basically create the entire emergency management system for our own purposes, without spending a whole lot of money and dealing with lousy WebEOC customer service.

I was the administrator of WebEOC, the useless software that we had to pay for, although it didn’t work for our practical purpose at all, and the company never worked with us to customize the software. At that time, I was regularly meeting with WebEOC administrators across the state. I was getting ready to take them with me. I would give them access to the software. They could use my template and customize it for their own jurisdiction. They would still be administrators but with better tools.

But alas, the Louisville government is a strange entity. They now use drones to spy on poor people, but not for search and rescue, or as an alternative communication method during disasters.

Final solution

PROTOTYPING, TESTING, PREPARATION...

Testing, prototyping

I used every opportunity to test my application for usability and intuitiveness. I would either do online moderated usability testing, or in person testing, both unmoderated and moderated.

That’s how I came up with the minimum viable product, and that’s how I made changes and added additional functionalities.

Preparation

Most of all, I was afraid of the negative and positive cloud judgement. I knew I had to prepare for it. With the negative cloud judgment, I expected that users would expect bureaucracy and unusable interfaces. With the positive cloud judgement, I knew when users see a beautiful interface, they seemed to gloss over functionalities. It’s a perception issue.

Showing up with straight talk

During the user onboarding sessions with kindergartens, I would explain the substance and purpose behind the project. I would reassure them that this was not just one more useless entanglement government is pushing down on them, and that they are all welcome to contribute with suggestions and collaboration methods. This seemed to work.

During my last field session, all kindergarten workers fired up about the safety issues they can encounter. They seemed to yearn for preparedness training and free communication channel with emergency services.

Dashing hopes with scope limitations

Some of the emergency managers requested that we include more functionalities in the emergency plans. While I recognized the benefit of required additions, I said no.

I said no out of compassion toward kindergarten workers. I said no out of respect for ChildCare Aware and the Division of ChildCare. Emergency management needed to be reminded that they were not in it alone.

Collaborating with like minded

People who cared about the safety of little ones gave their best to the project. Brummal Murphy from ChildCare Aware took it all the way to Frankfort and back, so the state leadership would understand and approve the project.

During the session with the Division of ChildCare, I shared about the purpose of the project and introduced them to using ChildSafe. We were 100 percent in tune.

Thank you

During the last two weeks, I invited kindergarten workers to come to our lab any time during the day. It was a perfect chance for me to observe and reassure. But it seems for some kindergarten users, it was a chance to be grateful.

“Thank you for doing this,” some would say, “it really makes our job easier.”

Some wanted to remind me about their abilities. “Just so you know, I’m not computer savvy.”

Then after completing the application and not asking for any help, they would say: “Well, it wasn’t hard at all.”

What did I learn?

A small group of like-minded people with different expertise can make miracles happen

  • Anything is achievable if you go step by step.
  • With each step, do your best and let go of the rest.
  • Limit yourself early, expand later.
  • Collaborate with those, who want to collaborate.
LIFE LESSON
Find your people

This awesome project opened my eyes about the type of work environment that suits me. The project succeeded because I collaborated with a small team dedicated to the mission (sounds simple, but it’s not). Each one pulled on their weight based on their expertise, but they were pulled forward because they actually cared about the common goal. Yes, we had a lot of missteps, but mostly because we didn’t operate from a safe space.
